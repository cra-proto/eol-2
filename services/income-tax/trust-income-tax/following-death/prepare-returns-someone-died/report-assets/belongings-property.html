Belongings, property and investment capital gains

Most belongings, investments, and property are deemed disposed upon the death of the individual. This means that any increase in its value since it was purchased is considered income that needs to be reported in the final return after the person dies.

If the deceased owned a house, or valuable belongings like art or financial investments, it must be assessed for capital gains.

Determine if property is rolled over to the surviving spouse or common-law partner

If the spouse or common-law partner is the beneficiary: Option 1 of 2

Any assets or property that are transferred within 36 months after the date of death to a surviving spouse or common-law partner that is resident in Canada can be excluded from the final return.

This effectively delays the tax implications until the spouse’s death.

If you choose not to use the spousal rollover or transfer assets after the 36 month deadline, you must assess them like all other transfers for capital gains.

If property or assets are not being transferred through spousal rollover: Option 2 of 2

Any property or assets will need to be assessed for capital gains in the final return.

Refer to the sections below on how to report types of capital gains and income on the final return.

Special circumstances

If you need more than 36 months

If you, as the legal representative of the deceased, cannot meet the 36 month condition in time, a request for an extension may be considered if you:

  • write a request to the director at your Tax Services Office
  • make the request before the end of the 36-month time period
Transfer to a testamentary spousal or common-law partner trust

As an alternative, you may roll the property over to a trust if:

  • The testamentary spousal or common-law partner trust is resident in Canada right after the property becomes locked-in for this trust.
  • The property becomes locked-in for the testamentary spousal or common-law partner trust no later than 36 months after the date of death. If you need more time to meet this condition, you can make a written request to the director at your tax services office before the end of the 36-month time period.

Calculate capital gains on the final return

Report on line
12700 and schedule 3 of the deceased's final income tax return

If there is no spousal rollover, the CRA considers that a person who died has disposed of all capital property right before death. The CRA calls this a deemed disposition.

You must calculate capital gains on belongings, property and investments for deemed disposition on death.

A capital gain or loss for deemed disposition at death is calculated as:

  • the proceeds of disposition (the fair market value of all belongings, investments, and property on the date of death)
  • minus the adjusted cost base (the original cost of belongings, investments, and property, plus any expenses to acquire them, such as commissions and legal fees)
  • equals capital gain to be reported in line 12700

Complete Schedule 3 based on the type of properties which the deceased taxpayer actually disposed or was deemed to have disposed from January 1 to right before death.

The following are the types of belongings, investments, and property you might need to assess for capital gains:

Real estate (other than a principal residence)

Real estate includes the following:

  • vacant land
  • rental property (both land and buildings)
  • farm property, including both land and buildings (other than qualified farm or fishing property)
  • commercial and industrial land and buildings

For each real property you sold in 2021 that includes land and a building, you must:

  • determine how much of the selling price relates to the land and how much is for the building
  • report the sale of your land and building separately on Schedule 3

To help you understand how to report a disposition of real property that includes land and a building, see the example Disposing of a principal residence partly used for earning income.

Contact us about special rules for properties owned by a deceased person before 1972

There are special rules for property that a deceased person owned before 1972. For details about these rules and for information about other property such as resource property or an inventory of land, contact the CRA at 1-800-959-8281.

Recapture of capital allowance and terminal loss

For depreciable property (usually capital property used to earn income from a business or property), in addition to a capital gain, there can also be a recapture of capital cost allowance or a terminal loss.

If you claimed Capital Cost Allowance on a rental property in previous year tax returns

In addition to calculating capital gains, you might need to calculate a Undepreciated Capital Cost (UCC) when you file a final return if you claimed a Capital Cost Allowance (CCA) in any previous year tax returns on that property.

You will need to compare the value of the property you claimed CCA for in previous tax years with the value of the property upon at the date of death.

This might result in either:

  • A recaptured CCA amount that goes on Line 9947 that you must repay the CRA, if the value of the property did not depreciate as much as you claimed for
  • A terminal loss amount that goes on Line 9948 that you can claim as part of the final return

For more details on how to calculate your Undepreciated Capital Cost, refer to column 2 in the T4036 - Rental Income guide

Valuable personal items

Capital gains must be reported in the personal income tax return 70(1) on belongings that increase in value, such as paintings or works of art, jewelry, stamp or coin collections, some types of automobiles, and antiques.

Investments

Capital gains on investments made outside of a registered plan or TFSA must be reported as part of the final return 70(1). This includes capital gains on:

  • shares
  • mutual funds
  • deposit certificates
  • treasury bills
  • bonds
  • GICs

You may not have to pay tax on the following properties, but you still have to report them:

Principal residence

Although gains on a principal residence can be exempt, you must still designate the principal residence in your return by completing both:

  • page 2 in Schedule 3, Capital Gains (or Losses) in 2021
  • Form T1255, Designation of a Property as a Principal Residence by the Legal Representative of a Deceased Individual

Special circumstances

Maintaining a principal residence status after the date of death

Generally, a principal residence no longer qualifies as a principal residence after the date of death.

The beneficiary of the estate might need to take into account capital gains from the date of death in future returns.

However, a property might qualify as the principal residence of a trust. Refer to 2.66.1 of the Income Tax Folio S1-F3-C2, Principal Residence.

Transfer to spouse or common-law partner

Where the principal residence is transferred to the deceased’s spouse, common-law partner, or testamentary spousal or common-law partner trust (under the conditions described in the next section), the CRA does not require you to make a principal residence designation for the period before death. Instead, the disposition must be reported and the designation may be made by the surviving spouse, common-law partner, or the trust at the earlier of the time of actual disposition or death of the surviving spouse.

A record should be kept of the years for which the deceased individual would have been eligible to make a principal residence designation for the particular property.

Personal-use belongings

When you sell personal-use property, such as cars and boats, in most cases you do not end up with a capital gain. This is because this type of property usually does not increase in value over the years. As a result, you may end up with a loss. Although you have to report any gain on the sale of personal-use property, generally you are not allowed to claim a loss.

For more information, see Personal-use property.

Farm and fishing land and supplies

If you have land or assets associated with farm or fishing that generated business income, you may be eligible to treat this as business farm or fishing property. Refer to farm or fishing property and equipment for both the special amount for transfer to a child and the capital gains deduction.

Qualified Small Business Corporation (QSBC) shares

If you have Qualified Small Business Corporation shares, you might be eligible for the capital gains deduction for any capital gains on these shares.

Find out how to apply the capital gains deduction.

A reserve brought into income from farm and fishing land and supplies or QSBC shares

You can only claim the deduction on reserves taken out of income from farm and fishing land and supplies or QSBC shares. In the final return, this only applies if you take reserves out of income with the joint election.

For further details on calculating capital gains for specific types of property, investments or belongings, refer to T4037 - Capital Gains - 2021

Reduce taxable income with net capital losses

Unlike in other years, net capital losses in the final return can also be applied to reduce other sources of taxable income. Refer to Net capital losses to find out how to claim them in the final return.

Reserves in the year of death

You can have reserves both on capital gains as well as self-employment income. You can generally see amounts of reserves claimed in prior years by reviewing prior year Notices of Assessment. This can be done in Represent a Client.

What is a reserve

When property is sold, sometimes some of the proceeds are not payable until after the year of sale. Usually, a person can deduct from income the part of the proceeds that are not payable until a later year. This is called a reserve.

Reserves claimed in year prior to death

Reserves claimed in the year prior to the deceased's death must be included in income on the final return, unless a joint election is filed on Form T2069.

Complete Form T2017 Summary of Reserves on Dispositions of Capital Property (even if it's for a reserve on self-employment income)

You will have to enter zero for the current yera amount of reserve for dispositions on lines 66840 or 66844 (section A), line 66850 (section B), line 66920 (section C) and line 66990 (section D), unless a reserve is being claimed in the year of death with the joint election in form T2069.

Eligible reserves in year of death

In the final tax return, you will not usually be able to deduct income from the reserve (amounts from a sale that will only be received in a future tax year).

However, there is an exception where you may claim the reserve in the final return. This is the case if a transfer to a spouse or common-law partner, or spousal or common-law partner trust, of the right to receive the proceeds of disposition or the income owing. When this happens, the legal representative and the beneficiary can choose to claim a reserve on the deceased's return.

To do this, you must meet the following conditions

  • the deceased was a resident of Canada on the date of death
  • the right to receive the proceeds of disposition or the income owing is transferred to the deceased's spouse or common-law partner, or to a spousal or common-law partner trust
  • if a transfer to a spouse or common-law partner, that person also has to have been a resident of Canada right before the deceased's death
  • if a transfer to a spousal or common-law partner trust, the trust has to be resident in Canada right after the proceeds or income become locked-in for the trust

To make the joint election, complete Form T2069, Election in Respect of Amounts Not Deductible as Reserves for the Year of Death, and attach a copy to the deceased's return.

Report sales after the death on a T3

If any property, investments or belongings are sold after the date of death, including the principal residence, before being passed onto a beneficiary, you must report the capital gain in line 1 of a T3 Trust Tax and Information Return for the estate.

The capital gain on the sale generally is the difference between the sale price and the fair market value (plus costs related to the disposition, if any) that you reported for the property, on the final return.
