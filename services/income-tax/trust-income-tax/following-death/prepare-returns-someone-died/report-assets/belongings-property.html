Belongings, property and investments for capital gains

Houses, valuable belongings, like artwork, collections or jewelry, as well as investments like mutual funds or cryptocurrency owned by someone who dies are taxable in the final return 70(1). This is called deemed disposition, since the CRA considered someone who died to have sold the belongings (even they didn't) when they die.

Only the increase in value since they were purchased or acquired (capital gain) is taxable. This means that any difference in its value since it was purchased is considered income that needs to be reported in the final return 70(1) after the person dies.

Although some properties like a primary residence are exempt from capital gains, you still need to attach a statement to declare the primary residence.

Determine if property is rolled over to the surviving spouse or common-law partner

If the spouse or common-law partner is the beneficiary: Option 1 of 2

Any assets or property that are transferred within 36 months after the date of death to a surviving spouse or common-law partner that is resident in Canada can be excluded from the final return 70(1).

This effectively delays the tax implications until the spouse’s death.

If you choose not to use the spousal rollover or transfer assets after the 36 month deadline, you must assess them like all other transfers for capital gains.

If property or assets are not being transferred through spousal rollover: Option 2 of 2

Any property or assets will need to be assessed for capital gains in the final return 70(1).

Refer to the sections below on how to report types of capital gains and income on the final return 70(1).

Special circumstances

If you need more than 36 months

If you, as the legal representative of the deceased, cannot meet the 36 month condition in time, a request for an extension may be considered if you:

  • write a request to the director at your Tax Services Office
  • make the request before the end of the 36-month time period
Transfer to a testamentary spousal or common-law partner trust

As an alternative, you may roll the property over to a trust if:

  • The testamentary spousal or common-law partner trust is resident in Canada right after the property becomes locked-in for this trust.
  • The property becomes locked-in for the testamentary spousal or common-law partner trust no later than 36 months after the date of death.
  • If you need more time to meet this condition:
    • write a request to the director at your Tax Services Office
    • make the request before the end of the 36-month time period

Calculate capital gains on the final return 70(1)

Report on line
12700 and schedule 3 of the deceased's final return 70(1)

If there is no spousal rollover, the CRA considers that a person who died has disposed of all capital property right before death. The CRA calls this a deemed disposition.

You must calculate capital gains on belongings, property and investments for deemed disposition on death.

A capital gain or loss for deemed disposition at death is calculated as:

  • the proceeds of disposition (the fair market value of all belongings, investments, and property on the date of death)
  • minus the adjusted cost base (the original cost of belongings, investments, and property, plus any expenses to acquire them, such as commissions and legal fees)
  • equals capital gain

You must complete Schedule 3 based on the type of properties which the deceased taxpayer actually disposed or was deemed to have disposed from January 1 to right before death. Schedule 3 will provide the amount to be reported on line 12700.

The following are the types of belongings, investments, and property you might need to assess for capital gains:

Real estate (other than a principal residence)

Real estate, other than the principal residence, might need to be reported in 2 different ways:

Personal-use property

Personal-use property includes some forms of property the deceased might have owned, such as:

  • a cottage
  • a secondary residence that is not used for rental purposes

Personal-use property should be reported on line 15800 of schedule 3, along with other valuable belongings.

You have a choice on which of the personal-use properties of the deceased to designate as the principal residence.

You cannot claim a loss on personal-use property.

Depreciable real estate and land

Real estate otherwise needs to be reported on lines 13599 and 13800 of schedule 3. Depreciable real estate and land includes:

  • vacant land
  • rental property (both land and buildings)
  • farm property, including both land and buildings (other than qualified farm or fishing property)
  • commercial and industrial land and buildings

For each real property you sold in 2021 that includes land and a building, you must:

  • determine how much of the selling price relates to the land and how much is for the building
  • report the sale of your land and building separately on Schedule 3

To help you understand how to report a disposition of real property that includes land and a building, see the example Disposing of a principal residence partly used for earning income.

Contact us about special rules for properties owned by the deceased before 1972

There are special rules for property that the deceased owned before 1972. For details about these rules and for information about other property such as resource property or an inventory of land, contact the CRA at 1-800-959-8281.

Recapture of capital cost allowance and terminal loss on rental property

Rental properties are considered depreciable property (capital property used to earn income from a business or property) and might have additional amounts to report for any recapture of capital cost allowance or terminal loss. Refer to property used to earn earn income from a business

Valuable personal items

Capital gains must be reported in the personal income tax return 70(1) on belongings that increase in value, such as paintings or works of art, jewelry, stamp or coin collections, some types of automobiles, and antiques.

  • Report personal-use property (furniture, cars, boats, cottages, etc) on line 15800 of schedule 3.
  • Report listed personal property (jewellery, stamp and coin collections, etc) on line 15900 of schedule 3.

Generally, you cannot claim a loss on personal-use property or on listed personal property.

When calculating the net gain or loss for each, however:

  • You are not allowed to offset personal-use property gains with personal-use property losses
  • You may offset listed personal property gains with listed personal property losses

For more details, refer to personal-use property in the T4037 Capital gains - 2021

Investments

Capital gains on investments made outside of a registered plan or TFSA must be reported as part of the final return 70(1). This includes capital gains on:

  • shares
  • mutual funds
  • deposit certificates
  • treasury bills
  • bonds
  • GICs

Report these dispositions on lines 15199 and 15300 of schedule 3.

Property used to earn income from business (depreciable)

Capital gains on depreciable property are reported like other types of depreciable real estate and land even if they are business property such as machinery or equipment.

Recapture of capital cost allowance and terminal loss

For depreciable property, in addition to a capital gain, there can also be a recapture of capital cost allowance or a terminal loss.

In addition to calculating capital gains, you might need to calculate a Undepreciated Capital Cost (UCC) when you file a final return 70(1) if Capital Cost Allowance (CCA) was claimed in any previous year tax returns on that property.

You will need to compare the value of the property that CCA was claimed for in previous tax years with the value of the property upon the date of death.

This might result in either:

  • A recapture of CCA goes on Line 9947 that you must repay the CRA, if the value of the property did not depreciate as much as you claimed for
  • A terminal loss amount that goes on Line 9948 that you can claim as part of the final return 70(1)

If you are reporting a recapture of CCA or terminal loss for a rental property:

  • You will report these amounts in a T776 Statement of Real Estate Rentals

  • For more details on how to calculate your Undepreciated Capital Cost, refer to column 2 in the T4036 - Rental Income guide

If you are reporting a recapture of CCA or terminal loss for business property:

  • You will report these amounts on line 8230 of T2125 Statement of Business or Professional Activities

  • For more details on how to calculate your Undepreciated Capital Cost, refer to T4002 - Self-employed Business, Professional, Commission, Farming, and Fishing Income

You may not have to pay tax on the following properties, but you still have to report them:

Principal residence

Although gains on a principal residence can be exempt, you must still designate the principal residence in your return by completing both:

  • page 2 in Schedule 3, Capital Gains (or Losses) in 2021
  • Form T1255, Designation of a Property as a Principal Residence by the Legal Representative of a Deceased Individual
What properties qualify to be designated as principal residence

You may only designate 1 property as the principal residence of the deceased in the final return 70(1).

The principal residence can be any of the following types of housing units:

  • a house
  • a cottage
  • a condominium
  • an apartment in an apartment building
  • an apartment in a duplex
  • a trailer, mobile home, or houseboat

A property qualifies as a principal residence for any year if it meets all of the following four conditions:

  • It is a housing unit, a leasehold interest in a housing unit, or a share of the capital stock of a co-operative housing corporation acquired only to get the right to inhabit a housing unit owned by that corporation
  • The deceased owned the property alone or jointly with another person
  • The deceased, or their current or former spouse or common-law partner, or any of their children lived in it at some time during the year
  • You designate the property as the principal residence

The land on which the home is located can be part of the principal residence. Usually, the amount of land that you can consider as part of the principal residence is limited to half of a hectare (1.24 acres). However, if you can show that the deceased needed more land to use and enjoy their home, you can consider more than this amount as part of the principal residence. For example, this may happen if the minimum lot size imposed by a municipality at the time the deceased bought the property is larger than half of a hectare.

Special circumstances

Maintaining a principal residence status after the date of death

Generally, a principal residence no longer qualifies as a principal residence after the date of death.

The beneficiary of the estate might need to take into account capital gains from the date of death in future returns.

However, in rare circumstances, a property might qualify as the principal residence of a trust. Refer to 2.66.1 of the Income Tax Folio S1-F3-C2, Principal Residence.

Transfer to spouse or common-law partner

Where the principal residence is transferred to the deceased’s spouse, common-law partner, or testamentary spousal or common-law partner trust:

  • a principal residence designation for the period before death is not required in the final return 70(1)
  • the deemed disposition of the property and any capital gain or loss is not required in the final return 70(1)

Instead, the spouse or common-law partner will need to make the principal residence designation and report any dispositions and capital gains or losses at the earlier of the time of actual disposition or death of the surviving spouse.

A record should be kept of the years for which the deceased individual would have been eligible to make a principal residence designation for the particular property.

Farm and fishing land and supplies

If the deceased had land or assets associated with farm or fishing that generated business income, you may be eligible to treat this as business farm or fishing property. Refer to farm or fishing property and equipment for both the special amount for transfer to a child and the capital gains deduction.

Qualified Small Business Corporation (QSBC) shares

If the deceased had Qualified Small Business Corporation shares, you might be eligible for the capital gains deduction for any capital gains on these shares.

Find out how to apply the capital gains deduction.

A reserve brought into income from farm and fishing land and supplies or QSBC shares

You can generally claim a deduction on reserves taken out of income from farm and fishing land and supplies or QSBC shares. However, in the final return 70(1), you can only take reserves out of income with the joint election.

For further details on calculating capital gains for specific types of property, investments or belongings, refer to T4037 - Capital Gains - 2021.

Reduce taxable income with net capital losses

Unlike in other years, net capital losses in the final return 70(1) can also be applied to reduce other sources of taxable income. Refer to Net capital losses to find out how to claim them in the final return 70(1).

Reserves in the year of death

When property is sold, sometimes some of the proceeds are not payable until after the year of sale. Usually, a person can deduct from income the part of the proceeds that are not payable until a later year. This is called a reserve.

You can have reserves both on capital gains as well as self-employment income.

Determine if the deceased had reserves that need to be reported

You can generally see amounts of reserves claimed in prior years by reviewing prior year Notices of Assessment. This can be done in Represent a Client.

Report reserves claimed in year prior to death on the final return 70(1)

Reserves claimed in the year prior to the deceased's death must be included in income on the final return 70(1), unless a joint election is filed on Form T2069.

Complete Form T2017 Summary of Reserves on Dispositions of Capital Property

For reserves on self-employment income, you will not have to complete this form, just include the reserve directly in the relevant line.

You will have to enter zero for the current year amount of reserve for dispositions on lines 66840 or 66844 (section A), line 66850 (section B), line 66920 (section C) and line 66990 (section D), unless a reserve is being claimed in the year of death with the joint election in form T2069.

Joint election to claim eligible reserves in the year of death

In the final return 70(1), you will not usually be able to deduct income from the reserve (amounts from a sale that will only be received in a future tax year).

However, there is an exception where you may claim the reserve in the final return 70(1). This is the case if the right to receive the proceeds of disposition or the income owing is a transferred to a spouse or common-law partner, or spousal or common-law partner trust, . When this happens, the legal representative and the beneficiary can choose to claim a reserve on the deceased's return.

To do this, you must meet the following conditions

  • the deceased was a resident of Canada on the date of death
  • the right to receive the proceeds of disposition or the income owing is transferred to the deceased's spouse or common-law partner, or to a spousal or common-law partner trust
  • if a transfer to a spouse or common-law partner, that person also has to have been a resident of Canada right before the deceased's death
  • if a transfer to a spousal or common-law partner trust, the trust has to be resident in Canada right after the proceeds or income become locked-in for the trust

To make the joint election, complete Form T2069, Election in Respect of Amounts Not Deductible as Reserves for the Year of Death, and attach a copy to the deceased's return.

Report sales after the death on a T3 return

If any property, investments or belongings are sold after the date of death by the trust or estate, you must report the capital gain in line 1 of a T3 return for the estate.

The capital gain on the sale generally is the difference between the sale price and the fair market value (plus costs related to the disposition, if any) that you reported for the property, on the final return 70(1).

Many of the forms available above for capital gains on the T1 final return 70(1) have equivalent forms for the T3 return:

  • Calculate a trust's capital gains in T3 Schedule 1 Dispositions of Capital Property
  • T3 Schedule 2 Reserves on Dispositions of Capital Property
  • T1079 Designation of a Property as a Principal Residence by a Personal Trust

For more detail on calculating capital gains in a T3 return, refer to:

  • T4013 Trust Guide 2021
  • T4037 Capital Gains 2021

Capital losses in the T3 Trust Income and Information return can be carried back to the final return 70(1)

Report sales after transfer to a beneficiary

If any property, investments or belongings are sold by the beneficary after the distribution of the estate, the beneficiary would need to report any increase in value compared to what is reported in the final return 70(1). For more details, refer to Property you inherit or receive as a gift.
