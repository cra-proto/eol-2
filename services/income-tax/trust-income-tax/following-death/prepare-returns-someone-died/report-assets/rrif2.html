RRIFs

How a Registered Retirement Income Fund (RRIF) is treated and reported at the time of the plan holder's (annuitant) death depends on whether there was a rollover or transfer to a survivor or beneficiary.

Report RRIF income and value

Income during the final year

Found on slip
T4RIF - box 18 (issued to the deceased annuitant)
Report on line
13000 of the deceased annuitant's Final return 70(1)

All amounts the annuitant received from the RRIF in the year of death will have to be reported on the annuitant’s final return 70(1). A T4RIF slip is issued in the name of the annuitant for the year of death and will also show any other amounts the annuitant received in the year.

If the deceased was 65 or older, or if the deceased was under 65 and received the RRIF payments due to the death of their spouse or common-law partner, see Line 11500 - Other pensions or superannuation.

Value of the RRIF on the date of death and rollovers to a spouse or common-law partner

At the time of the annuitant's death, we consider them to have received the fair market value of all property held in their RRIF.

You can partially or completely reduce the amount of income that is reported on the RRIF holder's final return 70(1) if amounts from the RRIF are transferred or rolled over to the registered plans of beneficiaries.

Is the RRIF being rolled over?

Yes - rollover to a spouse or common-law partner as successor annuitant
Found on slip
T4RIF - box 16 (issued to the spouse or common-law partner)
Report on line
11500 of the spouse or common-law's T1 Income Tax and Benefit return

If the annuitant's spouse or common-law partner is designated as a successor annuitant to all or part of the RRIF in the RRIF contract or the will:

  • the amount of the RRIF that is rolled over to the successor annuitant will not be reported in the final return of the deceased
  • the spouse or common-law partner will begin receiving amounts paid out from the plan and will report those as part of their own income

If a successor annuitant is not identified in the RRIF contract:

If the deceased's will states that the spouse or common-law partner is entitled to the amounts paid under the RRIF, or that the spouse or common-law partner is the sole beneficiary of the estate, that person and the legal representative can jointly elect, in a written letter, to treat the amounts paid to the estate as being paid to the spouse or common-law partner.

The RRIF carrier must also agree to continue the payments under the deceased annuitant's RRIF to the surviving spouse or common-law partner.

  • The written election has to specify that this person is electing to become the annuitant of the RRIF
  • Attach a copy of the written election to the income tax return of the surviving spouse or common-law partner

After the election is made, the spouse or common-law partner will be treated as if they were the beneficiary designated in the RRIF contract.

Yes - rollover to a spouse or common-law partner who is sole beneficiary of the RRIF
Found on slip
T4RIF - box 16 (issued to the spouse or common-law partner)
Report on line
11500 of the spouse or common-law's T1 Income Tax and Benefit return

If this rollover occurs, the RRIF is not reported on the deceased annuitant's final return 70(1).

The beneficiary must report the amount rolled over as income on their T1 Income Tax and Benefit return, and may be able to claim a deduction for some or all of this amount.

2 conditions must be met:

  • spouse or common-law partner is named as sole beneficiary of the RRIF contract, and
  • entire RRIF property is directly transferred, by December 31 of the year following the year of death, into a registered plan (RRSP, Pooled Registered Pension Plan (PRPP), Specified Pension Plan (SPP), or Registered Retirement Income Fund (RRIF)) in which the spouse or common-law partner is the annuitant, or to an issuer to buy an eligible annuity

If there is more than 1 beneficiary, or if the transfer doesn't happen by December 31, there is still the option to transfer to a qualifying survivor.

No - transfer to a spouse or common-law partner who doesn't qualify for a rollover, or a financially dependent child or grandchild (qualifying survivor)
Found on slip
T4RIF - box 16 (issued to the spouse or common-law partner)
Report on line
11500 of the spouse or common-law's T1 Income Tax and Benefit return

The beneficiary must report the amount transferred or rolled over as income on their T1 Income Tax and Benefit return, and may be able to claim a deduction for some or all of this amount.

If a qualifying survivor receives an amount from a deceased annuitant's RRIF that qualifies as a designated benefit, the annuitant's legal representative can claim a reduction to the amount that the annuitant is considered to have received at the time of death.

What qualifies as a designated benefit

Amounts transferred from the RRIF to a qualifying survivor, up to:

  • the fair market value of the RRSP on the day the annuitant died
  • generally, income earned in the RRSP from the day after the annuitant died to the date the RRSP property is distributed

For more information on refund of premiums, refer to Chart 1 - Amounts paid from a deceased annuitant's RRSP

If these amounts are paid to the annuitant's estate, they will qualify as a refund of premiums if both of the following conditions are met:

  • there is a qualifying survivor who is a beneficiary of the annuitant's estate
  • the annuitant's legal representative and the qualifying survivor jointly file Form T1090, Death of a RRIF Annuitant- Designated Benefit or Joint Designation on the Death of a PRPP Member, to designate all or part of the amounts paid to the estate as a refund of premiums received by the qualifying survivor
Which children qualify as financially dependent

A child or grandchild of an annuitant, where before the annuitant's death, were living with them and met 1 of the following conditions:

  • net income for the previous year (line 23600 of income tax return) was less than the basic personal amount (line 30000 of income tax return) for that previous year
  • financial independence is due to a mental or physical infirmity and your net income for the previous year was equal to or less than the basic personal amount, plus the disability amount (line 31600 of income tax return) for that previous year

The reduction, which is determined by filling out Chart 2, allows for a redistribution of the annuitant's income to the qualifying survivor who actually received it. This redistribution of income allows the deceased annuitant and the qualifying survivor to pay the least amount of tax the law allows.

When a qualifying survivor includes a designated benefit into income, they can defer paying tax on the amount by transferring it to an RRSP, PRPP, SPP, RRIF, Registered Disability Savings Plan (RDSP) or to an institution to buy an eligible annuity.

  • Transfers that qualifying survivors can chose

The transfer must be completed in the year the designated benefit is received or within 60 days after the end of the year.

If the qualifying survivor is 71 in the year the designated benefit is received, the transfer to an RRSP must be completed by December 31 of that year.

The issuer who receives the transferred funds will issue a receipt to the qualifying survivor. The qualifying survivor can use the receipt to claim a deduction on their T1 Income Tax and Benefit return for the year the refund of premiums was received.

Rollover to an RDSP
Found on slip
T4A - box 22 (issued to ........)
Report on line
13000 of the deceased annuitant's T1 Income Tax and Benefit return

The existing RRIF rollover rules allow a specified RDSP payment from the deceased person's RRIF to the RDSP of an eligible individual.

Who qualifies as an eligible individual

A child or grandchild of a deceased annuitant under an RRSP or a RRIF, or of a deceased member of a Registered Pension Plan (RPP), PRPP, or SPP who was financially dependent on the deceased for support, at the time of the deceased's death, because of an impairment in physical or mental function.

The eligible individual must also be the beneficiary under the RDSP into which the eligible proceeds will be paid.

A specified RDSP payment is a payment that meets all of the following conditions:

  • the payment is made to an RDSP under which the eligible individual is the beneficiary
  • the payment satisfies the contribution conditions described in the Registered Disability Savings Plan
  • effective March 19, 2019, if the eligible individual no longer qualifies for the disability tax credit, the payment is made before the end of the fifth taxation year throughout which the beneficiary is ineligible for the disability tax credit

Form RC4625, Rollover to a Registered Disability Savings Plan (RDSP) Under Paragraph 60(m), must be attached to both the deceased annuitant's and the eligible individual's income tax and benefit returns.

In these situations, you will not have to fill out a Schedule 7, RRSP, PRPP, and SPP Unused Contributions, Transfers, and HBP or LLP Activities. However, you must attach to the income tax and benefit return the receipt indicating the amount of the rollover.

The amount that can be rolled over to an RDSP cannot exceed the RDSP lifetime limit of $200,000.

Claim the deduction for amounts if rolled over or transferred

  • If the property was directly transferred to a beneficiary's RRSP, Pooled Registered Pension Plan (PRPP) or Specified Pension Plan (SPP), the beneficiary can claim a deduction equal to the amount transferred, on line 20800 of their T1 Income Tax and Benefit return.
  • If the amount was transferred to their Registered Retirement Income Fund (RRIF), Registered Disability Savings Plan (RDSP) or annuity, the beneficiary can claim a deduction equal to the amount transferred, on line 23200 of their T1 Income Tax and Benefit return

Change in value after the date of death

Sometimes there is an increase in the value of an RRIF between the date of death and the date of final distribution to the beneficiary or estate.

This amount has to be included in the income of the beneficiary or the estate for the year it is received. A T4RIF slip will be issued for this amount.

Was there a decrease in the value of the RRIF?

Yes - there was a decrease in the value

Sometimes, the fair market value of the property of an RRIF decreases between the date of death and the date of final distribution to the beneficiary or the estate.

  • If the total of all distributions from the RRIF is less than the fair market value of the property that was included in the deceased annuitant's income for the year of death, the deceased's legal representative can request that the difference be deducted on the deceased's final return through a reassessment.
  • Form RC249 - Post-Death Decline in the Value of a RRIF, an Unmatured RRSP and Post-Death Increase or Decline in the Value of a PRPP can be validated by the RRSP issuer and will allow the executor to claim a deduction on line 23200.
  • Generally, for the deduction to be allowed, the final distribution must occur by the end of the year that follows the year of death.
No - there was no change in the value

If the value of the RRIF did not change between the date of death and distribution to a beneficiary, there are no further amounts to report.
