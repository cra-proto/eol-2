CPP/QPP death benefit

The Canada Pension Plan/Quebec Pension Plan (CPP/QPP) death benefit amount received is reported by the beneficiary on their T1 Income Tax and Benefit return or by the estate on a T3 Trust Income and Information return. It is not reported on the final tax return 70(1) of the person who died.

Unlike a death benefit that an employer may pay to the estate or to a named beneficiary, the CPP/QPP benefit is not eligible for the $10,000 death benefit exemption.

Did the estate receive the CPP/QPP death benefit?

Yes and it is the only income of the estate
Found on slip
T4A (P)
Report on line
13000 of the beneficiary's T1 return

If the CPP/QPP death benefit is the only income of the estate and a T3 Trust Income Tax and Information return is not otherwise required to be filed, the beneficiary of the estate will receive a T4A (P) Statement of Canada Pension Plan Benefits slip.

This amount is reported on directly on the beneficiary's T1 Income Tax and Benefit return.

Yes and it is part of other income of the estate
Found on slip
T4A (P)
Report on line
47 or 19 of the T3 return for the year the payment was received

If the death benefit is payable to a beneficiary in the year, report the amount on line 47 of the T3 return and on line 926 of Schedule 9. The legal representative will also need to prepare and issue a T3 slip in the beneficiary's name and the beneficiary will be required to include the amount on their T1 Income Tax and Benefit return.

If the CPP/QPP death benefit is not payable to a beneficiary in the year it is received by the estate, the amount will be included in the estate’s taxable income reported on line 19 of the T3 Trust Income Tax and Information return in the year it is received by the estate and the estate will pay tax on that amount.

    Did a beneficiary receive the CPP/QPP death benefit?

    Yes, a beneficiary of the estate received the death benefit
    Found on slip
    T3 or T4A (P)
    Report on line
    13000 of the beneficiary's income tax return

    Beneficiary received the CPP/QPP death benefit through the estate

    If the estate received the CPP/QPP death benefit and it was part of other income after the person died, the legal representative must issue a T3 Statement of Trust Income Allocations and Designations to the beneficiary. The beneficary will then report that on their T1 Income Tax and Benefit return, not reported as part of the final return 70(1) of the person who died.

    Beneficary applied for the CPP/QPP benefit directly

    If the beneficiary applied for and directly received the CPP/QPP benefit, they will receive a T4A (P) Statement of Canada Pension Plan Benefits slip and report the amount on their personal tax return, not reported as part of the final return 70(1) of the person who died.

    For more detailed information, see Reporting a CPP/QPP death benefit amount.

    No, someone else received the death benefit
    Found on slip
    T4A (P)
    Report on line
    13000 of a T1 Income Tax and Benefit return

    The amount on the T4A (P) Statement of Canada Pension Plan Benefits slip is to be reported as part of the person who received the death benefit's T1 Income Tax and Benefit return, not as part of the final return 70(1) of the person who died.

    However, a death benefit will generally not be taxable if the recipient is not a beneficiary of the estate, and all of the following circumstances apply:

    • the taxpayer who received the death benefit paid the deceased's funeral expenses
    • the amount of the death benefit is not more than the funeral expenses
    • the deceased has no heirs, and there is no other property in the estate

    Death benefit from an employer

    A death benefit from an employer is an amount you may receive after a person's death for their employment service. The amount typically goes to a beneficiary, but can also go to the estate. Up to $10,000 of the death benefit is exempt from being taxed.

    Did beneficiaries receive a death benefit of more than $10,000?

    No, a beneficiary received less than $10,000

    You do not need to report.

    Up to to $10,000 of the total of all death benefits paid (other than CPP/QPP death benefits) is not taxable and does not need to be reported.

    Yes, a beneficiary received more than $10,000
    Found on slip
    T4A
    Report on line
    13000 of the beneficiary's income tax return

    All amounts over $10,000 are taxable and the amount on the T4A Statement of Pension, Retirement, Annuity, and Other Income slip needs to be reported by the beneficiary on their T1 Income Tax and Benefit return, not on the final return 70(1) person who died.

    For more information on how to report a death benefit that an employer pays on a personal tax return, see line 13000 - Other income - death benefits.

      Did the estate receive a death benefit of more than $10,000?

      No, the estate received less than $10,000

      You do not need to report.

      Up to to $10,000 of the total of all death benefits paid (other than CPP or QPP death benefits) is not taxable and does not need to be reported.

      Yes, the estate received more than $10,000
      Found on slip
      T4A
      Report on line
      line 11 of the T3 return

      The death benefit from an employer of the person who died is considered income after death, a T4A Statement of Pension, Retirement, Annuity, and Other Income is issued and a T3 Trust Income and Information Return needs to be filed.

      A trust fund for beneficiaries may have also been set up by the employer and may qualify as a death benefit.

      In both cases, the estate may be able to exclude up to $10,000 of the amount from income.

      Distributing to single or multiple beneficiaries

      If you distribute the total death benefit to a single beneficiary according to the provisions of the will, the beneficiary may be able to exclude up to $10,000 of the payment from income. Enter on line 935, the amount from line 926 on the T3 Trust Income and Information return eligible for this exclusion.

      If you distribute the total death benefit to more than one beneficiary, split the amount eligible for this exclusion among those beneficiaries. The total eligible amount that is split cannot exceed $10,000. The beneficiaries can use this information to calculate the taxable portion that they have to report on their personal tax returns.

      If you exclude the eligible death benefit from the estate's income, only the taxable portion flows out to the beneficiary. Report only the taxable portion of the death benefit on line 11 of the T3 Trust Income and Information return.

      No beneficiaries receive the death benefit

      If no one other than a estate received a death benefit, report the amount that is more than $10,000 on the T3. Even if the estate did not receive all of the death benefits in one year, the total tax-free amount for all years cannot exceed $10,000.

      To find out what to report if anyone else received a death benefit for the same person, see archived Interpretation Bulletin IT-508R, Death Benefits.
