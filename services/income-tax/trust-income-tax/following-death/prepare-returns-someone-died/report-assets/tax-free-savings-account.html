TFSAs

In general, you do not need to report TFSA amounts as income on the final return or any optional returns.

Any reporting and tax implications will depend on the deceased’s type of TFSA, the beneficiaries, and if any income was earned after the date of death.

You may need to contact your financial institution or investment broker to determine which type of TFSA the deceased held.

Determine how the TFSA will be passed on

Depending on who receives the TFSA after the death, there will be different tax implications. Determining the type of beneficiary is the first important step and can be affected by:

  • designations in the deceased holder's TFSA contract
  • the terms of the deceased holder's will (if there was one)
  • provincial or territorial succession legislation

If there is no successor holder or designated beneficiary in the TFSA contract or will, the TFSA property is directed to the deceased holder's estate and distributed in accordance with the terms of the will.

Rollover or transfer the TFSA

Rollover to a successor holder (spouse or common-law partner)

If the TFSA is rolled over to the spouse or common-law partner (survivor), it will continue as a Tax-Free Savings Account under the successor holder.

Its value, both at the date of the original holder’s death and any income earned after that date, have no tax implications and does not need to be reported.

This applies for all 3 types of TFSA accounts:

  • deposit
  • annuity contract
  • trust arrangement

If the successor holder already has their own TFSA, they would be considered the holder of 2 separate accounts if they choose not to consolidate them.

When the successor holder has ownership, they can make tax free withdrawals and new contributions, depending on their own unused TFSA contribution room. They could also transfer part of the value from one account to the other (a qualifying transfer) and it would not affect available TFSA contribution room.

A survivor may also designate a successor holder.

Transfer to a designated beneficiary (spouse or common-law who doesn't qualify for a rollover, qualified donee, or someone specifically named by the deceased)
Report on line
13000 of the beneficiary or the estate's income tax return

If funds from the TFSA account are transferred to the family or friends specifically named and documented by the deceased, then the the deposit or annuity contract (TFSA amounts) no longer exists in a TFSA and will no longer tax-free afer the date of death.

The fair market value of the TFSA at the time of death is not taxable. However, All earnings that grow after the holder's death will be taxable to the beneficiary and has to be reported by them as income.

Find out how to report income from investments on the beneficiary's personal income taxes:

  • Report income from dividends
  • Report interest and other investment income
  • Report capital gains

The beneficiary may contribute the amounts they receive to their own TFSA as long as they have the unused TFSA contribution room available. In this case, it would no longer be taxable as a part of their own TFSA.

Donation to a qualified donee (registered charity)

A qualified donee (registered charities, municipalities, etc) must transfer the funds within the 36-month period following the holder’s death.

Once the donation has been completed, it is possible to have the deceased’s final return reassessed, to be able to claim the charitable donation tax credit.

Different rules apply if the TFSA account is a trust arrangement
Found on slip
T4A - box 134 (issued to the beneficiary or the estate)
Report on line
13000 of the beneficiary or estate's income tax return

After the original holder’s date of death, the trust continues and remains non-taxable until the end of the exempt period.

Any amount up to the fair market value (FMV) of the deceased holder’s TFSA on date of death can be paid to beneficiaries without them having to report any amount as income.

Any amount paid to beneficiaries that represents an increase in the FMV after the date of death, is taxable to the beneficiaries and has to be reported by them as income.

The trust has the exempt period to distribute both the taxable and non-taxable amounts. The trustee will designate the part of each payment that represents non-taxable FMV at the date of death, with the remaining being taxable.

The exempt period is considered to start the day after the original holder’s death and goes until December 31 of the year following the death.

  • If the trust continues to exist beyond the end of the exempt period

    The trust will be taxable on any undistributed income and must file yearly, a T3RET, T3 Trust Income Tax and Information Return.

    The trust will also be required to prepare a T3, Statement of Trust Income Allocations and Designations, in that year or later years, for any distributions of taxable amounts to beneficiaries.

  • Example income after the death to report on the beneficiary's T1

    Martin's mother, who lived in a province that recognizes a TFSA beneficiary designation, passed away on January 9, 2021. The value of her TFSA on that date was $11,000. There was no excess TFSA amount in her account. In her TFSA contract, she had named Martin as the sole beneficiary. Her estate was settled on June 7, 2021. By that time, $200 in additional income had been earned and the full amount of $11,200 was paid to Martin.

    The value of Martin's late mother's TFSA as of the date of her death — $11,000, is not taxable. The income earned after the date of her death, $200, is taxable to Martin. He will receive a T4A slip showing this amount in box 134 "Tax-Free Savings Account (TFSA) taxable amount" in the "Other information" section. Martin can contribute any of the amounts he receives to his own TFSA as long as he has unused TFSA contribution room available.

Excess and exempt contributions to the TFSA

Excess contribution

You might need to make a repayment if the TFSA holds more than the allowed contribution room

If at the time of death, there is an excess TFSA amount in the deceased holder's TFSA, a tax of 1% per-month applies on the highest excess amount, for each month in which the excess stays in the TFSA, up to and including the month of death.

In these cases the legal representative must file 2 forms:

  • RC243 Tax-Free Savings Account (TFSA) Return
  • RC243-SCH-A Schedule A – Excess TFSA Amounts

Also, the successor holder is deemed to have made, at the beginning of the month following the date of death, a contribution to their TFSA that’s equal to the amount by which the excess TFSA amount is more than the total FMV, at the date of the holder's death, of all property under any arrangements that ceased to be a TFSA because of the holder's death.

If that contribution creates an excess TFSA amount in the successor holder's TFSA, they will be subject to the tax of 1% per-month on the highest amount for each month they have an excess contribution.

  • Example of excess amount taxed until it is repayed

    Jamal is 43 years of age and a Canadian resident. He opened his TFSA in 2009 and made the maximum contributions in each year. In 2020, Jamal made the following transactions during the year:

    • contribution on January 6 $5,000
    • contribution on March 10 $1,000
    • contribution on June 3 $2,700
    • withdrawal on October 2 $800

    Jamal's contribution room for 2020 was $6,000. The first contribution that created the excess TFSA amount was the $2,700 contribution on June 3rd. As of that date, his total contributions in 2020 were $8,700 ($5,000 + $1,000 + $2,700). This means that as of June 3, he had an excess amount in his TFSA of $2,700 ($8,700 of total contributions minus $6,000 of contribution room).

    Jamal had to pay a tax on his excess contributions. This tax was 1% of the highest excess TFSA amount in each month and applies until Jamal either withdraws the entire excess amount or until he becomes entitled to enough unused TFSA contribution room to absorb the excess.

    In this example, Jamal's tax was $173 for 2020, calculated as follows:

    • highest excess TFSA amount per month for January to May was $0. No tax is payable for those months
    • highest excess TFSA amount per month for June to October was $2,700. Tax of 1% per month on the highest excess amount was $135 ($2,700 × 1% × 5 months)
    • highest excess TFSA amount per month for November and December was $1,900. Tax of 1% per month on the excess amount was $38 ($1,900 × 1% × 2 months)

    Although Jamal withdrew $800 in October, the tax was calculated based on the highest excess TFSA amount in each month. The highest excess TFSA amount in October was still $2,700.

    For the months of November and December, Jamal still had an excess TFSA amount, but because of the withdrawal he made, his remaining excess TFSA amount for those last two months was $1,900 (the prior excess amount of $2,700 less the withdrawal of $800).

    Therefore, in total for 2020, his tax was $173 ($135 for June to October + $38 for November to December).

    At the beginning of 2021, Jamal's TFSA contribution room was $4,100 which is calculated as follows:

    • Jamal's 2020 and 2021 TFSA limits ($6,000 + $6,000)
    • less Jamal's contributions in 2020 ($8,700)
    • plus Jamal's withdrawals from the TFSA in 2020 ($800)

    The tax of 1% per month will continue to apply for each month that the excess TFSA amount stays in the TFSA. It will continue to apply until whichever of the following happens first:

    • the entire excess amount is withdrawn for eligible individuals
    • the entire excess TFSA amount is absorbed by additions to their unused TFSA contribution room in the following years

Exempt contribution

If designated as a beneficiary, the survivor has the option to contribute and designate all or a portion of a survivor payment as an exempt contribution to their own TFSA, without affecting their own unused TFSA contribution room.

For more information, refer to Designation of an exempt contribution by a survivor

Example of using the beneficiary's contribution room to offset an excess amount

Miriam and Pauline were a married couple. Each had available TFSA contribution room of $6,000 at the beginning of 2021. Miriam initially contributed $6,000 to her TFSA, and Pauline contributed $1,500 to hers. On June 12, 2021, Miriam contributed an additional $2,000 to her TFSA, bringing her total contributions for 2021 to $8,000.

As Miriam only had contribution room of $6,000 for 2021, she had an excess TFSA amount of $2,000. Miriam passed away on September 18, 2021, and the value of her TFSA on that date was $8,000. Miriam had named Pauline as the successor holder of her TFSA if she dies. As Pauline meets all the conditions to be considered a successor holder, she becomes the holder of the TFSA from September 18, 2021.

Since an excess TFSA amount remained in Miriam's TFSA at the time of her death, Pauline is deemed to have made, as of October 1, 2021, a $2,000 contribution to her TFSA (which is the excess amount in Miriam's TFSA). As Pauline had only previously contributed $1,500 to her own TFSA, she still had unused TFSA contribution room for 2021 of $4,500. As a result, the $2,000 deemed contribution does not create an excess TFSA amount in her account.

Therefore, there are no tax consequences to Pauline based on this deemed contribution. Her unused contribution room for the rest of 2021 is $2,500. However, the legal representative of Miriam's estate must file Form RC243, Tax-Free Savings Account (TFSA) Return, and Form RC243-SCH-A, Schedule A – Excess TFSA Amounts, for the 2021 tax year reporting the excess in Miriam's TFSA for the period from June up to and including September 2021.

Survivors and beneficiaries not living in Canada

Any payments of amounts earned above the fair market value (FMV) from the deceased holder's TFSA, and made out to a non-resident beneficiary or non-resident survivor during the exempt period, are reported on an NR4 slip, ‘Statement of Amounts Paid or Credited to Non-Residents of Canada, and are subject to non-resident withholding tax.
