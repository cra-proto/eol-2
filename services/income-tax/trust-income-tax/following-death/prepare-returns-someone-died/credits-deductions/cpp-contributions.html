CPP and QPP contributions

In addition to the usual calculations for CPP and QPP contributions, there are some additional steps when calculating for the final return of someone who died.

On this page

  • Determine where to calculate and report
  • Prorate the maximum pensionable earnings and the basic exemption
  • Claim CPP or QPP deduction on optional returns

Determine the calculation method

The calculation you will make depends on whether the deceased had employment or self-employment income and whether they contributed to both the CPP and QPP or just CPP.

  • Use schedule 8 of the T1, if you only need to calculate CPP contributions.
  • Use RC381 Inter-provincial Calculation for CPP and QPP Contributions and Overpayments, if you need to calculate both CPP and QPP contributions.

Both schedule 9 and RC381 use the same section numbering:

Employment income
Calculate in Part 3

Report on lines:

  • 22215, 30800, and 44800
Self-employment income
Calculate in Part 4

Report on lines:

  • 22200, 31000, and 44800
Both income types
Calculate in Part 5

Report on lines:

  • 22215, 30800, 22200, 31000, and 44800

Prorate the maximum pensionable earnings and the basic exemption

In the year of death, the maximum pensionable earnings and the basic exemption for the calendar year are prorated. These amounts are found on the monthly propration table in the .

The proration calculation for a deceased person who did not stop receiving CPP or QPP disability benefits or, for CPP, did not revoke a previous election to stop making CPP benefits during the year:

  • Maximum pensionable earnings or basic exemption
  • divided by 12 (months in a calendar year)
  • multiplied by Number of months from January up to and including the month of death
  • equals Prorated maximum pensionable earnings or basic exemption
Example of how to prorate the CPP or QPP earnings and exemption

If the date of death is in May, for 2021, the maximum pensionable earnings would be $25,667 ($61,600 divided by 12 and multiplied by 5 months) and the basic exemption would be $1,458 ($3,500 by 12 and multiplied by 5 months).

If the deceased had been receiving CPP or QPP disability benefits but stopped receiving them in February the number of months used in the prorating calculation would be 4 as CPP/QPP contributions were not required in January.

If the deceased person had revoked a previous election to stop making CPP contributions in February, the number of months used in the prorating calculation for CPP would be 3 as CPP contributions were not required for January and February.

Claim CPP or QPP deduction on optional returns

You may be able to claim CPP and QPP deductions of the deceased on an optional return if:

  • Employment income is claimed on an optional return for rights or things 70(2)
  • Self-employment income is claimed on an optional return for a partner or proprietor 150(4)

You generally only claim deductions for CPP/QPP contributions on employment income, lines 22215 and 30800 on the return for rights or things 70(2), and for self-employment income, lines 22200 and 31000 on the return for a partner or proprietor 150(4). Overpayment line 44800 can be associated with either form of income.

How to calculate amounts to claim on the optional return

The amounts allowed in each of lines 22215, 22200, 30800, 31000,42100 and 44800 is the calculated maximum amount minus what was reported on other returns.

The amount allowed in the previous returns is determined by the prorated maximum pensionable earnings and the basic exemption.

Overpayments

Overpayments to CPP or QPP are reported on 44800.

If there are only employment earnings, overpayments are any negative amounts you calculate by subtracting the total required contributions from the total contributions withheld.

If there are self-employed and other earnings and the calculated required contributions is a negative amount, the overpayment is 50% of the negative amount.
