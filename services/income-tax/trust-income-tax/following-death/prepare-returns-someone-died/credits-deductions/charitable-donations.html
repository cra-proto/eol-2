Donations and gifts

A tax credit can be claimed in the final return for charitable donations the deceased or their spouse or common-law partner made before the date of death in the final return. As well, donations made by the estate may be claimed on the T3 return.

On this page

  • Donation made before the date of death
  • Donation made after death by will or designation by the estate
  • Gifts of belongings, property, or art

Donation made before the date of death

Claim on line
34900 of the deceased annuitant's final income tax return

Complete Schedule 9, Donations and Gifts to determine the amount you should claim on line 34900. As long as the deceased or their spouse or common-law partner did not previously claim the amounts, you can claim donations to qualified donees from up to 5 years before the death.

Support the claims for donations and gifts with official donation receipts that the qualified donee has issued, showing either the deceased's name, or the deceased's spouse's or common-law partner's name.

  • Find out which organizations are considered qualified donees
  • How to complete schedule 9, donations and gifts

Charitable donations cannot be carried forward from a T1 return to a T3 return.

Claim limit for donations in the final return

The amount of the gift(s) that may be claimed on the deceased’s final return for purposes of the tax credit may be up to whichever of the following amounts is less:

  • the eligible amount of the gift(s), made in the year of death (this may include gifts made by a GRE or a former GRE), plus the unclaimed portion of the eligible amount of any gifts made in the five years before the year of death
  • 100% of the deceased's net income on line 23600 on the return

Claim a donation on an optional return

You can split donations claimed between the final and any optional returns you are eligible to file.

The amount that can be claimed on any of the returns cannot be more than net income reported on that return.

The total charitable donations across the final and optional returns cannot be more than the eligible amount of charitable donations.

Donation made after death by will or designation by the estate

Claim on line
34900 of the deceased annuitant's final income tax return or 30 of schedule 11A of the T3

Donations made by will and designation donations are deemed to be made by the individual's estate after the death when the donation is transferred to the donee.

What is a designation donation

A designation donation is a donation of a direct distribution of proceeds to a qualified donee from an RRSP (including a group RRSP), RRIF, a tax-free savings account (TFSA), or a life insurance policy (including a group life insurance policy) as a result of a beneficiary designation.

This does not apply if the qualified donee is the policyholder or an assignee of the deceased person’s interest in the policy.

A testamentary trust, whether it is a Graduated Rate Estate (GRE) or not, can claim a donation tax credit for a donation in the year in which the donation is made or in any of the five following years.

Donations made before 2016 follow different rules

Donations resulting from a will that occur before 2016 were deemed to be made immediately before the death and are reported differently. Refer to the T3 trust guide for the year the donation was made for more details on how to report these amounts.

How to claim in the T3 return

Estate donations generally need to be claimed in a T3 trust income tax return by completing schedule 11A.

Claim limit for donations in the T3 return

The amount of the gift(s) that may be claimed on the estate's return for purposes of the tax credit may be up to whichever of the following amounts is less:

  • the eligible amount of the gift(s), made in the trust's tax year (this may include gifts made by the estate up to 5 years previous, but may not include donations from before the death)
  • the sum of:
    • 25% of taxable capital gains from the donation of capital property, less any capital gains deductions claimed against that property
    • 25% of the recaptured of capital cost allowance reported as a result of making the gift
    • 75% of the trust's net income
Exceptions for gifts of ecologically sensitive land made after February 14, 2014

Gifts of ecologically sensitive land made after February 14, 2014 have some exceptions. They can be:

  • included in the T3 return as long as they were made up to 10 years before the trust's tax year
  • the claim limit based on 75% of the trusts's net income does not apply to ecologically sensitive gifts of land
Spousal sharing is not allowed

Surviving spouse or common-law partners are not allowed to report on their own tax returns donations made by the deceased's estate.

How to carry back donations to the final return

Under certain conditions, a donation by the estate can be carried back to the final return of the deceased. To be eligible to carry back the donation:

  • The estate must meet all the requirements of a GRE except for the 36-month time limit
  • The gift must be made within 60 months (5 years) after the date of death
  • The donated property or proceeds must have been acquired by the estate on and as a consequence of the death (or property that was substituted for such property)

If you carry back the donation to the final return, ensure that you:

  • Include the total donation amount in Schedule 11A of the trust return for that tax year
  • Subtract the amount being applied to the previous year return in line 4 of Schedule 11A
  • Report the amount being carried back in the final return (or other eligible tax return), generally Schedule 9 and line 34900 of the T1 return

Make sure you include the official donation receipt with both returns.

Documentation if you don't have an official donation receipt

For gifts that have not yet been received by the qualified donee, provide a copy of all of the following:

  • the will
  • a letter from the estate to the charitable organization that will receive the gift, advising of the gift, a description of the property being gifted and its estimated value
  • a letter from the charitable organization acknowledging the gift and stating that it will accept the gift
  • a statement or letter from the legal representative of the estate stating all of the following:
    • the estate is the graduated rate estate (GRE) of the deceased individual and will be designating itself as such
    • the estate intends to make the gift within 60 months after the date of death
    • the amount of the gift claimed on the final return of the deceased individual will not be claimed on any other return (of any estate of the deceased individual in any taxation year)
Carry the donation back to a different tax year

Subject to the same conditions, a GRE donation can alternatively be carried back to:

  • an earlier taxation year of the GRE
  • the year immediately preceding the death

Gifts of belongings, property, or art

For non-cash gifts, you must include the value of the future gift can be reasonably ascertained and supported.

Report capital gains or losses if donated after the date of death

If the estate donates property and the property's fair market value upon transfer to the qualified donee has increased or decreased since the disposition reported in the final return, the gain or loss in value will generally be recognized for income tax purposes. This applies both to Graduated Rate Estates and former Graduated Rate Estates.

Donating certified cultural property

If the property is certified cultural property or ordinarily benefits from a capital gains inclusion rate of zero, see the sections called “Gifts of certified cultural property” and “Capital gains realized on gifts of certain capital property” in Pamphlet P113, Gifts and Income Tax. The same treatment will apply to the capital gains on the deemed disposition of the property immediately before the individual’s death if the property donated meets both of the following conditions:

  • The property benefits from a capital gains exemption or exclusion when donated as described in the sections of Pamphlet P113 referenced above.
  • The estate is a GRE and the donation is a GRE donation.

Additional special rules exist for the proceeds of disposition and cost amount of gifts of art, from the artist’s inventory, on and as a consequence of the artist’s death.

Claim limit on the fair market value of gifts of property

There is a claim limit to the value of property gifted in a donation if:

  • the donated property was acquired as part of a gifting arrangement that is a tax shelter
  • the property is being gifted otherwise than as a consequence of the taxpayer's death, and the property was acquired less than 3 years, or in some cases, less than 10 years, before making the gift

For a gift of property made to a qualified donee, in these circumstances, the limit to the eligible amount of the gift that can be used in computing the donation tax credit amount is the lesser of the property's:

  • FMV otherwise determined
  • cost (or its adjusted cost base if it is capital property, or adjusted cost basis if it is a life insurance policy at the time the gift was made)

The limitation on the eligible amount of a gift will not apply to gifts of any of the following:

  • inventory
  • real property or an immovable property located in Canada
  • certified cultural property (unless it was acquired as part of a gifting arrangement that is a tax shelter)
  • certified ecologically sensitive land including a covenant, an easement, or in the case of land in Quebec, a real servitude (or for gifts made after March 21, 2017, a personal servitude when certain conditions are met)
  • a share, debt obligation, or right listed on a designated stock exchange
  • a share of the capital stock of a mutual fund corporation
  • a unit of a mutual fund trust
  • an interest in a related segregated fund trust
  • a prescribed debt obligation
  • shares of controlled corporations in certain circumstances

For more information about charitable donations and the special rules that may apply, see the Federal Income Tax and Benefit Guide, Pamphlet P113, Gifts and Income Tax, or Income Tax Folio S7-F1-C1, Split-receipting and Deemed Fair Market Value.
